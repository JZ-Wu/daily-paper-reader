Abstract 

Multimodal large language models have made rapid progress in video understanding, yet existing benchmarks largely rely on simple prompts and provide limited evidence about whether models can satisfy explicit output con-straints. We introduce VCIFBench, a bench-mark for evaluating complex instruction fol-lowing in video understanding. VCIFBench constructs constraint-rich instructions from both benchmark-adapted and directly video-grounded prompts, covering content, format, style, and structure requirements, and evalu-ates model outputs with a hybrid verification pipeline. The benchmark contains 306 satisfi-able test instructions, a 540-pair DPO prefer-ence dataset, and a 30-item conflict diagnos-tic subset. Experiments on 10 MLLMs show that joint constraint satisfaction remains chal-lenging. We further show that DPO training on VCIFBench data can improve instruction-following performance. 

1 Introduction 

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have shown strong capabilities in understanding im-ages, videos, and other heterogeneous inputs (Dai et al., 2023; Comanici et al., 2025). A growing set of benchmarks evaluates multimodal reason-ing, long-video understanding, and general multi-modal competence (Yue et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024b; Chang et al., 2024). How-ever, these benchmarks mainly assess task-level performance under relatively simple prompts, leav-ing unclear whether MLLMs can reliably follow complex instructions with explicit constraints in video-grounded settings. This gap matters in practical use. A model of-ten needs not only to understand what happens in a video, but also to satisfy user requirements on output content, format, style, and structure. Such requirements are especially important in agentic or tool-use settings, where schema violations or un-controlled outputs may cause downstream failures (Zhang et al., 2025a). Although instruction follow-ing has been widely studied in language models (He et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024), multimodal instruction-following benchmarks re-main more limited, often focusing on images (Ding et al., 2025; Bitton et al., 2023) or narrow video tasks such as captioning (Li et al., 2025). Such benchmarks capture only part of the broader task design space. Practical video instructions may also require prediction, temporal or causal reasoning, option selection, or structured generation. As a result, constraints such as length limits, evidence requirements, and ordering rules can behave differ-ently across task types and lead to different failure modes. Evaluation is another challenge. Rule-based checks are reliable for programmable requirements such as length, format, or exact surface forms (Zhou et al., 2023a), but they cannot cover many se-mantic or video-grounded constraints. LLM-based judging is more flexible, but may introduce biases such as length or positional bias (Fu et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024a). A useful benchmark therefore needs both diverse constraint coverage and reason-ably reliable verification. We introduce VCIFBench , a benchmark for 

Video Complex Instruction Following. VCIF-Bench constructs constraint-rich instructions from both benchmark-adapted prompts and directly video-grounded prompts, using videos from Tem-pCompass (Liu et al., 2024c), MMWorld (He et al., 2025), NExT-QA (Xiao et al., 2021), and YouCook2 (Zhou et al., 2017). The benchmark covers 20 task types and 40 constraint types across content, format, style, and structure. The current release includes 306 satisfiable test instructions, a 540-pair DPO preference split, and a separate 30-item conflict diagnostic subset for unsatisfiable constraint combinations. 1

To evaluate model outputs, we develop a hy-brid verification pipeline that combines rule-based verification, hybrid parameter extraction with ex-ecutable checkers, and LLM-based judging. For semantic content constraints, we further decom-pose high-level requirements into targeted sub-constraints, enabling finer-grained diagnosis of video-grounded failures. Experiments on 10 pro-prietary and open-source MLLMs show that re-liable joint constraint satisfaction remains chal-lenging, especially for video-grounded content constraints. 2 Related Work 

2.1 Multimodal Large Language Models and Benchmarks 

Recent multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have demonstrated strong capabilities in understanding and reasoning over images, videos, and other heterogeneous inputs (Dai et al., 2023; Comanici et al., 2025). To evaluate these capabilities, many multimodal benchmarks have been proposed, covering settings such as multimodal reasoning (Yue et al., 2024), long-video understanding (Zhou et al., 2025), spatial reasoning (Guo et al., 2025), and video summarization (Jung and Kim, 2025). These benchmarks have been valuable for tracking model progress and exposing failure modes across tasks and modalities. Many multimodal benchmarks still emphasize task-level performance under relatively simple prompts, offering limited insight into whether mod-els can satisfy fine-grained requirements on content, format, style, and structure (Fu et al., 2024a). This motivates our focus on complex instruction follow-ing in video settings, where task completion must be coupled with explicit constraint satisfaction. 

2.2 Instruction Following 

Instruction following has been studied extensively in language models. Early work focused on ex-plicit and verifiable requirements. For example, IFEval (Zhou et al., 2023a) evaluates programmat-ically checkable format constraints, while more recent benchmarks study complex instruction fol-lowing across diverse task types and composition patterns, including combinations of semantic and format constraints (He et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024). EifBench further considers settings with multiple instructions and multiple constraints (Zou et al., 2025). Prior work has also explored meth-ods for improving compliance in such scenarios, including in-context learning (Zhou et al., 2023b), supervised fine-tuning (Zhang et al., 2025b), and DPO (Ding et al., 2025). In contrast, multimodal instruction following re-mains less developed, particularly for video. Ex-isting studies mostly examine image-based set-tings (Ding et al., 2025; Bitton et al., 2023), while 2video introduces temporally distributed evidence and more varied response goals. IF-VidCap (Li et al., 2025) is an important step toward video-based instruction following, but its scope is mainly caption-oriented. Less is known about how mod-els handle constraints across broader video tasks, such as prediction, option selection, reasoning, and structured response generation. Our work studies this broader setting by combining diverse video task types, multiple constraint categories, and a hybrid evaluation pipeline with rule-based, exe-cutable, and judge-based checks. 

3 VCIFBench 

3.1 Data Collection 

We construct VCIFBench from four complemen-tary video sources, focusing on short videos with durations under two minutes: TempCompass (Liu et al., 2024c), MMWorld (He et al., 2025), NExT-QA (Xiao et al., 2021), and YouCook2 (Zhou et al., 2017). These sources cover diverse video scenarios and reasoning demands: TempCompass provides temporal and state-change cases, MMWorld cov-ers scientific and general video-understanding sce-narios, NExT-QA contributes causal and temporal reasoning over daily activities, and YouCook2 pro-vides procedural cooking videos. The satisfiable test split contains 306 items. The overall construc-tion and evaluation pipeline is shown in Figure 1. 

3.2 Instruction Generation Task taxonomy. We organize video-grounded tasks into three broad categories: perception , rea-soning , and expressive generation . Unlike bench-marks that mainly evaluate task completion, VCIF-Bench evaluates whether models can satisfy ex-plicit constraints while performing the task. De-tailed task definitions and examples are provided in Appendix A.7. 

Constraint taxonomy. We build on prior tax-onomies of practical instruction constraints (Jiang et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025) and adapt them to video instruction following. As shown in Figure 2, VCIFBench contains 40 con-straint types organized into four dimensions. For-mat constraints specify surface requirements such as serialization, length, and fixed patterns. Con-tent constraints require models to select, include, exclude, or emphasize video-supported informa-tion. Style constraints control communicative form, such as tone, target audience, persona, and rhetoric. Format

Structure constraints specify organization require-ments, such as chronological, causal, or hierarchi-cal ordering. Importantly, although content-related constraints depend on video understanding, faith-fulness to the video content is itself a core aspect of instruction following, as a helpful response must remain grounded in the video rather than rely on unsupported assumptions. 

Compositional instruction types. Following prior work (Wen et al., 2024), we construct instruc-tions with several composition patterns: And for jointly satisfying multiple constraints, Chain for combining subtasks, Selection for conditioning the response on explicit alternatives, and Nested for hi-erarchical combinations. We do not report separate performance for these patterns because they are not mutually exclusive; for example, And commonly appears inside Chain and Selection instructions. 

Instruction construction. We construct instruc-tions from two complementary sources, using GPT-5.2 (Singh et al., 2026) as the primary generator. 

Benchmark-derived prompts. For source datasets with QA-style task descriptions, we rewrite suitable prompts into open-ended video tasks and combine them with compatible con-straints. We discard cases that cannot be mean-ingfully transformed, such as questions asking about content absent from the video. TempCom-pass also provides original–reverse video pairs, which naturally support binary Selection instruc-tions and allow us to test whether models follow 3video-grounded alternatives rather than passing a single branch by chance. 

Direct video-grounded prompts. For videos without suitable annotations, we select tasks com-patible with observable video evidence and then compose them with candidate constraints. GPT-5.2 generates and revises the instruction to improve fluency. We further use Gemini-2.5 Pro (Comanici et al., 2025) to check whether content-related con-straints are supported by the visible video, followed by manual revision to correct residual inconsisten-cies. Appendix A.2 reports the full constraints map. 

3.3 Evaluation 

VCIFBench uses a hybrid evaluation pipeline be-cause constraints differ in how directly they can be verified. 

Rule-based verification. For constraints with de-terministic criteria, such as JSON validity or re-quired surface forms, we directly implement check-ing functions and compute binary outcomes. 

Hybrid verification. Some constraints are rule-checkable only after their parameters are identified from the instruction. For example, a word-count constraint requires extracting the target range be-fore applying a checker. Following prior work on constrained and tool-use evaluation (Zhong et al., 2025; Wen et al., 2024), we ask the judge model to extract the required parameters from the instruction and then execute the corresponding checker. 

LLM-based judging. For constraints that de-pend on semantic interpretation, discourse organi-zation, or video-grounded reasoning, we use GPT-5 (Singh et al., 2026) to produce binary constraint-level judgments, following prior work on LLM-as-a-judge, multimodal LLM-based evaluation, and complex instruction evaluation (Zheng et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2025; Wen et al., 2024). To make video-grounded evaluation more reliable, we further decompose content-related constraints, such as summarization, focus, tracking, and con-tent inclusion, into explicit evidence-grounded sub-constraints. We report judge–human alignment in Appendix A.5.

3.4 DPO Dataset Construction 

We construct a 540-pair DPO preference split from the same instruction construction pipeline. Chosen responses are generated by GPT-5.2 and then pro-cessed through the same evaluation pipeline and manual revision to ensure constraint satisfaction and video grounding. The rejected responses are constructed in two ways. Inspired by MM-IFEval (Ding et al., 2025), we first remove the explicit constraints from each instruction and ask MiniCPM-o-4.5 to answer the underlying video task, producing plausible but constraint-insensitive responses. Because this strat-egy may introduce superficial length or style dif-ferences, we also generate targeted negatives by prompting MiniCPM-o-4.5 to corrupt one specific constraint in the chosen response while preserv-ing the rest of the answer as much as possible. 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup 

Our final satisfiable test set contains 306 samples. We evaluate 10 multimodal large language models (MLLMs): Gemini-3-Pro (Gemini Team, Google DeepMind, 2025b), Gemini-3-Flash (Gemini Team, Google DeepMind, 2025a), GPT-5.4 (OpenAI, 2026), Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B (Bai et al., 2025), Qwen3-VL-8B (Bai et al., 2025), InternVL3.5-14B (Wang et al., 2025), InternVL3.5-8B (Wang et al., 2025), MiMo-VL-7B-RL (Team et al., 2025), MiniCPM-o-4.5 (Cui et al., 2026), and GLM-4.1V-9B-Thinking (Team et al., 2026). Table 1 reports in-struction pass rate (IPR), constraint pass rate (CPR), and failure severity on Base-306. We evaluate 10 MLLMs under the same input setting for the main results. All videos are uni-formly sampled at 0.5fps and capped to 1080p, with temperature set to 0.1 for generation. 

4.2 Main Results 

Table 1 reports the Base-306 results. The lead-ing proprietary models form a close group, with IPR around 52% but CPR around 85–86%. This gap shows that satisfying individual constraints does not reliably translate into satisfying the full instruction: errors accumulate when all constraints must be satisfied jointly. Across dimensions, Style and Structure are relatively strong for proprietary models, while Content remains lower at around 70– 71%. Format is also not saturated, indicating that even explicit surface requirements, such as word-count limits, JSON or CSV formatting can still fail under multi-constraint settings. Open-source models show substantially lower instruction-level success. Most achieve CPR around 69–74%, but their IPR remains only around 24–29%, suggesting that they can satisfy many individual constraints but are less stable when constraints are composed. InternVL3.5-8B is the clearest outlier, with low scores across all dimensions. Since IPR only indicates whether an instruction fails, we further report near-miss rate and the av-erage number of violated constraints per failed in-struction. The severity metrics show that failures are not homogeneous. For Gemini-3-Pro and GPT-5.4, more than 73% of failed instructions are near misses, and each failed instruction violates only 1.44 and 1.35 constraints on average. By contrast, mid-tier open-source models have lower near-miss rates and higher violation counts. Thus, stronger models often remain close to satisfying all require-ments, whereas weaker models more often exhibit cascading failures across multiple constraints. Manual inspection supports this distinction. Gemini and GPT failures are usually narrow bound-ary errors: the semantic answer is often coherent and video-grounded, but one explicit constraint boundary is violated, such as a word budget, a CSV/JSON rule, a missing visual detail, or an ex-cluded extra detail. Some semantic judgments are inherently more interpretation-sensitive, so strong models may be closer to the practical ceiling on these cases. However, many remaining failures are still concrete compliance errors, suggesting that multi-constraint instructions place additional pres-sure even on otherwise simple constraints. In con-trast, InternVL3.5-8B failures more often indicate global breakdowns, including mixed languages, an-swering both branches of a selection instruction, or uncontrolled long continuations. 

Option-order robustness. We also test selection robustness by swapping the display order of Op-tion A and Option B. This diagnostic asks whether models follow the video-grounded branch condi-tion, rather than relying on the surface position or label of an option. As shown in Table 2, Gemini-3-Pro and GPT-5.4 are much more stable under option-order swaps. By contrast, Qwen3-VL-8B and MiniCPM-o-4.5 show much lower robustness: they sometimes select the correct path under one option order, but fail to preserve the same decision after the options are swapped. 4.4 Constraint Failure Mechanisms 

We further analyze why models fail on Base-306. Rather than treating all failed instructions as the same type of error, we distinguish between con-straints that are difficult in themselves and con-straints that become fragile mainly when composed with other requirements. This distinction helps explain why relatively high constraint-level per-formance does not necessarily translate into high instruction-level success. 

From hard constraints to failure mechanisms. 

The hardest constraints in Base-306 come from several sources. Some require video-grounded ev-idence selection and semantic compression, such as summarize , content_inclusion , and focus .Others involve negative or boundary control, such as content_exclusion and lexical_diversity ,where the model must avoid prohibited or repeated information while still producing a useful response. We also find that seemingly simple surface require-ments, such as word_count and csv , can remain fragile, especially for weaker models. 

Interaction patterns. We further examine whether some co-occurring constraints fail more often than expected from their individual failure rates. This analysis is diagnostic, since constraint types are not randomly assigned and some pairs have limited unique samples. Still, manual inspection suggests several recurring patterns. • Length/count budget × required content or structure. Length and count constraints become fragile when paired with content or structure re-quirements. Models often exceed the budget to include visual evidence, causal links, or re-6quired markers; conversely, they may satisfy the budget by omitting required visual anchors. This pattern suggests a trade-off between being complete and staying within strict output limits. • Selection branch. selection_accuracy can act as a gatekeeper constraint. Once a model chooses the wrong branch, preserves both branches, or leaves option labels in the answer, subsequent constraints often fail as a cascading consequence. • Style or rhetoric × rigid surface format. Per-sona, tone, and rhetorical constraints can en-courage greetings, labels, explanatory framing, or narrative openings. These additions may improve naturalness, but they can violate ex-act openings, fixed closings, plain-text require-ments, or label placement. In creative writing or role-play tasks, models may produce more readable responses while breaking exact surface constraints. • Output language/rhetoric × lexical exclusion or diversity. A model may avoid the exact pro-hibited word while reintroducing the excluded content through paraphrase, adjacent concepts, or plausible elaboration, especially when in-structed to produce the response in another lan-guage. Overall, Base-306 failures should not be inter-preted as isolated constraint errors. Some con-straints are intrinsically difficult because they re-quire video-grounded evidence selection or seman-tic compression. Others become difficult mainly under composition, where content, format, style, and task-specific response tendencies compete with one another.

4.7 Preference Optimization Results 

We train Qwen3-VL-8B on the 540-pair DPO split to test whether preference optimization can im-prove complex video instruction following. Train-ing uses LoRA with rank r = 16 , batch size 1, gra-dient accumulation 8, and learning rate 1 × 10 −5.Table 5 compares the DPO-tuned model with the original Qwen3-VL-8B on Base-306. DPO improves instruction-level success from 27.12% to 33.01%, a gain of 5.88 points. The constraint-level gains are also consistent: CPR-micro increases from 74.94% to 78.63%, and CPR-macro increases from 75.76% to 80.40%. The im-provement is observed across all four constraint dimensions. 

5 Conclusion 

We present VCIFBench , a benchmark for evaluat-ing complex instruction following in video under-standing, together with a construction pipeline and a hybrid evaluation pipeline. Experiments on 10 MLLMs show that even capable models still strug-gle to satisfy multiple explicit constraints jointly: substantial weaknesses remain in fine-grained for-mat control, adherence to video-grounded content, and robustness under instructions that combine multiple explicit constraints. We further show that DPO training on data derived from our pipeline can improve overall instruction-following performance. We hope VCIFBench will serve as a useful step toward more reliable, controllable, and practically deployable multimodal systems. 86 Limitations 

First, our current benchmark primarily centers on English instructions and outputs. Broader mul-tilingual coverage would be a useful extension for studying complex video instruction following across languages and linguistic conventions. Second, VCIFBench intentionally covers a fo-cused subset of video instruction following. Con-sequently, VCIFBench should be viewed as a con-trolled stress test for constraint-rich video instruc-tion following. Its results are informative about whether models can jointly satisfy explicit require-ments, but should not be used as a standalone mea-sure of overall video understanding, factual ground-ing, or deployment safety. Finally, our evaluation pipeline combines rule-based verification, hybrid verification, and LLM-based judging to balance coverage and reliability. Although this design improves practical coverage, variations in judge behavior and interpretation can-not be fully ruled out, especially for more semantic or subjective constraints. 

Potential Risks. VCIFBench is a controlled eval-uation resource and does not introduce direct de-ployment risks. A possible indirect risk is that optimizing models primarily for explicit constraint satisfaction could encourage formal compliance at the expense of broader reliability, such as factual grounding, safety, or appropriate refusal behavior. We therefore recommend using VCIFBench along-side broader evaluations of video understanding, factuality, and safety.

A.2 Base-306 Constraint Composition 

Figure 6 shows the distribution of the number of constraints per satisfiable instruction in Base-306. Empty outputs and API-blocked outputs are counted as failures: in our runs, two Gemini test items were blocked by the API safety filter, and five InternVL3.5-8B items produced empty outputs. 

A.3 Conflict Diagnostic Subset 

In addition to the 306 satisfiable test items, we con-struct a separate 30-item conflict diagnostic subset. Each item is derived from an existing instruction: we preserve the video-grounded core task, keep 2–4 otherwise normal constraints, and inject ex-actly one explicit incompatibility between two con-straints. The subset is balanced across ten conflict templates, with three items per template. For evaluation, Basic pass requires explicit un-satisfiability detection and conflict mention. Strict 

pass additionally requires localizing the conflicting requirements and not proceeding with an ordinary video answer. The full judge prompt is shown in Figure 16. 

A.4 Residual Failure Modes in Instruction Construction 

After automated verification, we manually inspect residual inconsistencies in the satisfiable split. De-spite multi-stage filtering, several recurring failure modes remain. First, some instructions contain mismatches be-tween content-related constraints and visual evi-dence, e.g., requiring details or events that do not appear in the video. We also observe occasional accidental cross-constraint conflicts, such as exact-word requirements that are incompatible with the required output language. These accidental cases are removed or repaired in the satisfiable split; by contrast, the separate conflict diagnostic subset in Appendix A.3 deliberately contains hard conflicts for diagnostic evaluation. Second, some Selection -type instructions re-main underspecified. In these cases, the unse-lected branch is not described clearly enough, or the branching condition is framed too subjectively (e.g., whether the response should be written “as a news report” or “as a tutorial”), rather than being tied to an objectively identifiable alternative in the video. This weakens the intended design of Selec-tion , where the correct branch should be uniquely determined by visual evidence. 

A.5 Human Validation and Judge Alignment 

To assess the reliability of our semantic evaluation pipeline, we conducted an additional human valida-tion study with three undergraduate annotators who had no prior experience with VCIFBench. The an-notators were compensated at a rate of USD 9 per hour and labeled 630 constraint-level validation instances. The annotators reached 96.19% com-plete agreement, with Fleiss’ κ = 0 .9342 . We use their majority vote as the human reference label for judge-alignment analysis. Because our main judge uses a closed-source model, we further examine whether an alternative judge model can serve as a more reproducible op-tion for deployment or independent replication. Ta-ble 8 compares GPT-5 and GLM-4.5V against the human majority vote. GPT-5 shows stronger align-ment with the human reference, reaching 88.89% agreement and κ = 0.7300. GLM-4.5V obtains lower but still moderate alignment, with 84.92% agreement.
